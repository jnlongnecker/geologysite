Seismic Waves

  • 2 types of waves to look at
    • P waves
    • S waves
  • Waves go faster in higher densities
    • This means as waves go further down, they speed up
  • Waves can be used to identify stuff underground
    • Waves are generated and reflect off of layers differently into receivers
      • Reflect: when a wave impacts matter and changes direction at an equal but opposing angle
      • Refract: when a wave impacts matter and maintains direction, but changes angle
    • On water, the wave receivers are called hydrophones
    • On land, the wave receivers are called seismographs
  • Seismic refraction refers to waves "bending" as they go further into the earth
    • As mentioned earlier, waves speed up in higher densities. So as the wave speeds up, it gets refracted over and over
    • This gives it its bending motion, and speeding up makes it bend up
      • he
    • We can tell where the division between the crust and the mantle is using this as well
    • Using a seismograph, where the slope changes, that indicates where the division is (called Mohorovicic Discontinuity)
    • This is because of the different makeup of the crust and mantle. Waves move faster through the mantle
  • P waves can go through all matter, while S waves can only travel through solids
    • If we used seismographs all over the world and sent out tons of S waves, we could map out where there was no solid matter in the interior
    • The S waves will travel normally through the solid part of the earth and be blocked by the interior liquid
    • This allows us to map where the core is (since the outer part is liquid) using S waves
    • Notice the P waves with a sharper bend, because they move faster
    • Using P waves, you can tell the inner core is solid because they travel differently in solids
    • You can also tell where the boundaries between layers are, as P waves also travel slightly differently through each layer
    • Finally, you can also tell where plates are subducting because the plate is "colder" and so the P waves travel slower through the cold material

Plate Tectonics

  • The main thing we're focusing on in this is the concept of Paleomagnetism
    • This is the study of remnant magnetism preserved in rocks to determine information about the earth's magnetic field
    • First, lets explain how a magnetic field is generated
      • Step 1: get a bunch of metallic material together. Preferably iron
      • Step 2: run an electrical current around the metal stuff
      • This causes the metal stuff to become a dipole (has a positive and negative end) and generates a magnetic field
      • The field comes out at the south pole and returns to the north pole
    • This part is important: Think of the magnetic field as a sheet of rays coming out of the core and then bends back into the core
      • Now imagine a rock on the surface of the earth. The magnetic field can be imagined as coming through the rock as it travels on its journey to bend back into the core
      • When a rock cools past a certain point (called its Curie Point) and it has lots of iron in it, it becomes magnetised in the same angle as the magnetic "ray" coming through it
      • This angle coincides with its latitudinal position on the earth
        • So a rock magnetised at 30°N latitude is magnetised at an angle of 30°
        • This angle is called the angle of inclination
        • This allows us to identify what latitude a rock was magnetised, but since there are north and south hemispheres we don't know which hemisphere just from the angle.
        • Main thing to take away: rocks record their originating latitude and the location of the poles at the time they originated
        • We know the direction of the north pole this way: if a rock has a 30° angle, the north pole is 60° away. If it has a 70° angle, it's 20° away and so on
    • Polar Wandering
      • If you record where the north pole was throughout time on different continents, you notice both that they don't line up and it changes througout time
      • If you reassembile the continents though, the paths do line up
      • This is evidence that the continents were moving, causing this phenomenon
    • Magnetic Reversals
      • Throughout time, the poles have switched (noth pole becomes south pole), called a geomagnetic reversal
      • This scale shows 9 geomagnetic reversals throughout time (count the lines between the boxes)
      • This scale is easily shown in oceanic crust in the form of stripes of different magnetism
  • Now lets talk about how to find out how fast the plates have moved
    • First, some terminology:
      • Isochrones - the black lines on the map. Signify 5 million years
      • Velocity - the measure of distance traveled over time
    • Since the time is the same between isochrones (5 MA), the further the distance between them, the faster the plate was moving
    • The spots where the black lines are the furthest apart was where the plate was moving the fastest
    • Using this, we can identify a few things here:
      • The fastest the plate was moving was at around 60 MA
      • The plate began to speed up about 90-85 MA
      • The plate slowed down about 45 MA (not coincidentally, this is the same time that India collides with the Eurasian Plate forming the Himalayas)
    • We can also look at the direction of plates using hotspots
      • Hotspots are still, while the plate is not
      • Hawaii is formed by a hotspot, and since the older islands are to the south-east, we know the plate is moving north-west
      • Try this: take a pencil and a piece of paper. The pencil represents the hotspot, the paper is the plate. Press the pencil into the paper and move the paper, keeping the pencil still.
      • The mark that the pencil makes is the "track" of molten rock left behind by the hotspot. Use this if you get confused

Flooding and Rivers

  • Flooding occurs when there is more water than the channel can contain, the channel being a river/stream/lake/hole in the ground
    • We're going to be looking at rivers here
  • First some terminology
    • Infiltration: the amount of water that goes into the ground. This is reduced with urbanization (more concrete and stuff)
    • Runoff: the amount of water that doesn't go into the ground. This is what causes flooding
    • Drainage basin: the area drained by a river at a specific spot. (AKA watershed)
      • Imagine you have a bowl, and the bowl has a hole in the bottom. The drainage basin of the hole is the entire bowl
    • Drainage divide: a seperator for drainage basins
      • These are the edges of your bowl. So if you have multiple bowls lined up, the edges of the bowl are your drainage divides
      • The Rocky Mountains make up the Continental Divide, which is a big ol' drainage divide
    • Discharge: the rate of flow (how much water [in volume] goes past a point over an amount of time)
      • This is what determines if an area floods or not
      • If you have high discharge, the channel gets rid of the water before it can accumulate and flood (which is good)
    • Floodplain: The water level at a given stage of flooding
  • The above graphic is called a hydrograph, which plots discharge over time
    • A stage is like a marker for discharge. At x stage, discharge is at least y amount.
  • A few things to point out
    • Discharge will rise rapidly, but it will fall slowly
    • There is a lag in the discharge amount compared to rainfall because of the time it takes for water to accumulate
  • Effects of urbanization
    • Lag time is reduced
    • Discharge amount is increased
    • The rising limb, as a result of lag time being reduced, is much steeper
    • Why: as mentioned before, there is less infiltration so more water is runoff (accumulates faster and in higher amounts)
  • Flood Frequency
    • Recurrence Interval - average number of years between events of similar or greater magnitude
      • A 100 year flood has a Recurrence Interval (RI) of 100
      • A 25 year flood has a RI of 25, etc
    • Exceedence Probability - the probability of a flood of a particular magnitude being equaled or exceeded in any given year
      • Exceedence Probability (p) of a 100 year flood is 1/100, or 1%
      • Exceedence Probability of a 25 year flood is 1/25, or 4%
      • Meaning, in any year we have a 4% chance of a 25 year flood and a 1% chance of a 100 year flood
  • Reducing Flooding
    • Now we need to put some concepts together
    • To reduce flooding, we want to keep discharge the same, because discharge is what is getting rid of the water
    • The cross-sectional area increases as flooding happens, because the water level rises. We want this to be as low as possible, because that will keep water levels low
    • Recall that Discharge = (cross-sectional area)*(velocity)
    • If we want to keep discharge the same and reduce cross-sectional area, we need to increase velocity
    • We can do this in 3 ways:
      • Decrease flow resistance
      • Increase channel efficiency
      • Increase slope
    • Manning's Equation
      • V = Average water velocity. Recall that velocity = distance/time
      • n = Mannings Roughness Coefficient (a fancy number for how resistant to flow the channel is)
      • R = Hydraulic Radius (another fancy number for how efficient the channel cross section is)
      • S = Slope, appoximately the slope of the channel
      • Key Observations
        • n is in the denominator, so making the channel less rough will increase velocity
          • This is done by basically adding concrete to the bottom to make it literally smooth
        • R and S are in the numerator, so increasing them increases velocity
        • Making the channel more efficient should intuitively make the channel flow faster
          • Measured by area/wetted perimeter
          • Wetted Perimeter: the part of the channel touching the water
          • Basically, just make the channel deeper and you make this more efficient
        • Similarly, making the channel steeper will make it go faster as well
          • This is done by decreasing the sinuosity
          • Sinuosity is a measure of how much the channel is meandering, or deviating from a straight line
          • As we learned in geometry, the fastest way from point A to point B is a straight line, so the more a channel deviates from that the slower it goes
          • This is measured by the (channel distance)/(linear distance)
  • Alluvial Streams
    • 2 Kinds
      • Braided Stream: a network of streams that branch and reunite
      • Meandering Stream: A stream having a pattern of meanders (bends, curves, loops or turns)
    • We're going to focus on Meandering Streams
      • Again, more terminology
        • Cutbank: the portion of a stream that is being eroded by faster moving water
        • Point bar: the portion of a stream that deposition (dropping of sediment) occurs due to slower moving water
          • A meandering river will have a point bar directly opposite to its cutbank
        • Lateral Migration: the process of a river migrating in the direction of its cutbank over time
        • Meander Cutoff: when two points of a river migrate back together and their point bars cut off the old river path
        • Oxbow Lake: the part of the river that is cut off in a meander cutoff
