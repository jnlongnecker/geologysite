Concept Checks and Review Questions

Chapter 1

    Concept Checks
  • 1.2 - List at least three different geologic hazards.
    Show Answer

    Volcanoes, floods, tsunamis, earthquakes, and landslides.

  • 3.2 - Summarize the basic steps followed in many scientific investigations.
    Show Answer

    A question is raised, observations are gathered, a hypothesis is developed and tested

  • 5.1 - What is a system? List three examples.
    Show Answer

    A group of interacting parts that form a complex whole.
    Ex: car's cooling system, transportation system, weather system.

  • 5.2 - What are the two sources of energy for the Earth system?
    Show Answer

    The Sun and the interior of the Earth.

  • 6.2 - List the inner planets and outer planets. Describe basic differences in size and composition.
    Show Answer

    Inner planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars - Small and solid.
    Outer planets: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune - Large and gaseous.

  • 7.1 - List and describe the three major layers defined by their chemical composition.
    Show Answer

    Crust - Oceanic and Continental Crust
    Mantle - Upper and Lower Mantle
    Core - Inner and Outer core, mostly iron

  • 7.2 - Contrast the lithosphere and asthenosphere.
    Show Answer

    Lithosphere: Solid, cool and rigid outer layer; likened to ice
    Asthenosphere: Fluid, weak mid layer

  • 7.3 - Distinguish between the outer core and the inner core.
    Show Answer

    Outer Core: Liquid iron responsible for Earth's magnetic field
    Inner Core: Solid iron due to intense pressure

  • 8.2 - Sketch and label a basic rock cycle. Make sure to include alternate paths.
    Show Answer

Chapter 2

    Concept Checks
  • 2.1 - What was the first line of evidence that led early investigators to suspect that the continents were once connected?
    Show Answer

    The plates of the contenents could fit together like a puzzle.

  • 2.2 - Explain why the discovery of the fossil remains of Mesosaurus in both South America and Africa, but nowhere else, supports the continental drift hypothesis.
    Show Answer

    It is unlikely it could have crossed the ocean, so it is more likely the continents drifted apart over time after the fossils were left.

  • 4.2 - Compare and contast the lithosphere and the asthenosphere (not an error, literally the same question as before).
    Show Answer

    Lithosphere: Solid, cool and rigid outer layer; likened to ice
    Asthenosphere: Fluid, weak mid layer

  • 4.4 - List the three types of plate boundaries and describe the relative motion at each of them.
    Show Answer

    Divergent - Plates move away from each other.
    Convergent - Plates move towards each other.
    Transform - Plates slide alongside one another.

  • 5.1 - Sketch or describe how two plates move in relation to each other along divergent plate boundaries.
    Show Answer

  • 5.4 - Briefly describe the process of continental rifting. Where is it occurring today?
    Show Answer

    When a divergent boundary is under continental crust, it pulls apart the crust and creates a rift.
    This is currently occuring in Africa (East African Rift).

  • 6.1 - Explain why the rate of lithosphere production roughly balances with the rate at which it is destroyed.
    Show Answer

    At the same time as new crust is being created, old crust is subducting into the mantle and melting.

  • 6.2 - Compare a continental volcanic arc and a volcanic island arc.
    Show Answer

    Continental - Results in volcanoes on the surface at oceanic-continental convergent boundaries.
    Island - Results in an island chain in the ocean at oceanic-oceanic convergent boundaries.

  • 6.3 - Describe the process that leads to the formation of deep-ocean trenches.
    Show Answer

    Oceanic crust subducts under continental crust, creating a deep trench where the two meet.

  • 6.4 - Why does the oceanic lithosphere subduct, while the continental lithosphere does not?
    Show Answer

    The oceanic lithosphere is much denser than the continental lithosphere, causing it to sit lower and subduct more easily.

  • 6.5 - Briefly describe how mountain belts such as the Himalayas form.
    Show Answer

    When continental crust subducts under continental crust, the less dense crust is thrust upwards creating mountains.

  • 7.1 - Sketch or describe how two plates move in relation to each other along a transform plate boundary.
    Show Answer

  • 7.2 - Differentiate between transform faults and the two other types of plate boundaries.
    Show Answer

    Transform faults occur where the plate shears and two plates rub against one another, while the other two the entire plate collides or separates.

  • 10.1 - Describe slab pull and ridge push. Which of these forces appears to contribute more to plate motion?
    Show Answer

    Slab Pull: When oceanic lithosphere subducts, it exerts a pull on the section not subducting.
    Ridge Push: Gravity pull down on elevated ridge crust, exerting a push on the rest of the crust from falling rock.
    Slab pull contributes more to plate motion.

  • 10.2 - What role are mantle plumes thought to play in the convective flow in the mantle?
    Show Answer

    Mantle plumes supply the upward motion of the mantle, while subducting crust supplies the downward.

    Review Questions
  • 1a - What observation led Alfred Wegener to develop his continental drift hypothesis?
    Show Answer

    The observation that the continents could fit together like a "puzzle".

  • 2 - Referring to the accompanying diagrams that illustrate the three types of convergent plate boundaries, complete the following:
      a. Identify each type of convergent boundary.
      b. Volcanic island arcs develop on what type of crust?
      c. Why are volcanoes largely absent where two continental blocks collide?
      d. Describe two ways that oceanic-oceanic convergent boundaries are different from oceanic-continental boundaries? How are they similar?
    Show Answer

    A: Oceanic-Continental, Oceanic-Oceanic, Continental-Continental
    B: Oceanic crust from oceanic-oceanic convergent boundaries
    C: There is not a lot of rising magma from melting lithosphere, because no water is present to lower the melting point
    D: Volcanoes grow from the ocean floor, oceanic-continental volcanoes produce mountains instead of islands. They are similar in that both produce volcanoes, a trench, and one plate subducts under the other.

  • 4 - Refer to the accompanying hypothetical plate map to answer the following question:
      b. Are continents A, B, and C moving toward or away from each other? How did you determine your answer?
      c. Explain why active volcanoes are more likely to be found on continents A and B than on continent C.
    Show Answer

    B: They are moving away from each other. There is a ridge between all 3, meaning there is a divergent plate boundary.
    C: There is subduction on A and B and no subduction on C, so A and B would have volcanoes from rising magma.

  • 7 - Australian marsupials (kangaroos, koala bears, etc.) have direct fossil links to marsupial opossums found in the Americas. Yet the modern marsupials in Australia are markedly different from their American relatives. How does the breakup of Pangaea help to explain these differences?
    Show Answer

    After Australia broke away from the Americas, the marsupials environment changed and their evolutionary path differed.

  • 8 - Density is a key component in the behavior of Earth materials and is especially important in understanding key aspects of plate tectonics. Describe three different ways that density and/or density differences play a role in plate tectonics.
    Show Answer

    Density differences causes subduction, less dense material from the mantle rises to create new crust at divergent plate boundaries, dense subducting material causes slab pull which drives plate motion.

  • 9 - Refer to the accompanying map and the city combinations given below to complete the following:
      (Boston, Denver), (London, Boston), (Honolulu, Beijing)
      a. List the cities (in pairs) that are moving apart as a result of plate motion.
      b. List the cities (in pairs) that are moving closer as a result of plate motion.
      c. List the cities (in pairs) that are presently not moving relative to each other
    Show Answer

    A: (London, Boston)
    B: (Honolulu, Beijing)
    C: (Boston, Denver)

Chapter 3

    Concept Checks
  • 1.1 - List five characteristics an Earth material must have in order to be considered a mineral.
    Show Answer

    1. Naturally Occurring
    2. Generally Inorganic
    3. Solid
    4. Orderly Crystalline Structure
    5. Definite Chemical Composition that allows for Some Variation

  • 1.2 - Based on the definition of a mineral, which of the following materials are not classified as minerals and why: gold, water, synthetic diamonds, ice, and wood
    Show Answer

    Gold - It chemical composition allows for no variation
    Water - It is not a solid
    Synthetic Diamonds - It is not naturally occurring
    Ice - It does not have an orderly crystalline structure
    Wood - It is organic

  • 1.3 - Definine the term rock. How do rocks differ from minerals?
    Show Answer

    Rock - Any solid mass of mineral or mineral like matter that occurs naturally as part of the planet. Rocks are made of minerals, and so do not have the same 5 defining traits.

  • 3.1 - What is the difference between an atom and an ion?
    Show Answer

    An ion is an atom with a charge, either negative or positive.

  • 3.2 - What occurs in an atom to produce a positive ion? A negative ion?
    Show Answer

    Positive: An atom gives up one or more valence electrons to another atom.
    Negative: An atom receives one or more valence electrons from another atom.

  • 3.3 - Briefly distinguish between ionic and covalent bonding and the role that electrons play in each type of bond.
    Show Answer

    Ionic Bonding: Oppositely charged ions attract to one another due to having more or less electrons.
    Covalent Bonding: Atoms share valence electrons and this force holds them together.

  • 5.2 - Why is color not always a useful property in mineral identification? Give an example of a mineral that supports your answer.
    Show Answer

    Due to slight differences in chemical composition, a mineral may have wildly different coloration. An example of this is quartz.

  • 5.4 - What differentiates cleavage from fracture?
    Show Answer

    Cleavage is a mineral's tendency to break at planes of weak bonds. Fracture is where extreme force has broken a mineral from an irregular plane.

  • 6.2 - Name a mineral other than olivine that contains two elements that readily substitute for one another. What are these two elements?
    Show Answer

    Pyroxene, Silicon and Oxygen

  • 7.2 - List the eight most common elements in Earth's crust
    Show Answer

    Oxygen, Silicon, Aluminum, Iron, Calcium, Sodium, Potassium, Magnesium

  • 8.2 - Draw a sketch of the silicon-oxygen tetrahedron
    Show Answer

  • 8.4 - What differences in silicate structure account for the different properties of quartz and talc?
    Show Answer

    Talc is a sheet silicate, while quartz lacks cleavage planes by having strong bonds in all directions.

Chapter 4

    Concept Checks
  • 1.1 - What is magma? How does magma differ from lava?
    Show Answer

    Magma is melted, fluid rock that is below the Earth's surface. Lava is magma once it comes onto the Earth's surface.

  • 1.2 - List and describe the three components of magma.
    Show Answer

    Liquid (melt) - mobile tons of the 8 most common elements in the crust
    Solid - Silicate crystals
    Gas (volatiles) - form a gas at surface pressure (water, carbon dioxide, sulfur dioxide)

  • 1.3 - Describe the process of crystallization.
    Show Answer

    Cooling reverses the melting process and chemical bonds return, forming crystals

  • 1.4 - Compare and contrast extrusive and intrusive igneous rocks.
    Show Answer

    Intrusive rocks form below the surface, while extrusive rocks form above the surface. Both are igneous rocks.

  • 2.1 - Igneous rocks are composed mainly of which group of minerals?
    Show Answer

    Silicates

  • 3.1 - Define texture.
    Show Answer

    The overall appearance of a rock based on the size, shape and arrangement of mineral grains.

  • 3.2 - How does the rate of cooling influence crystal size? What other factors influence the texture of igneous rocks?
    Show Answer

    Slower cooling produces larger crystals. The presence of gasses, if it was ejected from an eruption, and how much fluid is in the magma.

  • 3.3 - List the six major igneous rock textures.
    Show Answer

    1. Glassy
    2. Porphyritic
    3. Phaneritic
    4. Vesicular
    5. Pyroclastic
    6. Aphanitic

  • 3.4 - Explain how the crystals in pegmatites are able to grow so large.
    Show Answer

    The presence of fluids make it easier for ions to move, resulting in larger crystals.

  • 3.5 - What does a porphyritic indicate about the cooling history of an igneous rock?
    Show Answer

    The magma cooled slowly, then suddenly finished cooling quickly.

  • 4.1 - The classification of igneous rocks is based largely on two criteria. Name these criteria.
    Show Answer

    Texture and Mineral Composition

  • 4.2 - How are granite and rhyolite different? In what wway are they similar?
    Show Answer

    Granite is a phaneritic rock, while rhyolite is aphanitic. Both are felsic rocks with similar composition.

  • 4.3 - Classify each of the following as felsic, intermediate, or mafic: gabbro, obsidian, granite, and andesite.
    Show Answer

    Felsic: Granite, Obsidian
    Intermediate: Andesite
    Mafic: Gabbro

  • 4.4 - Describe each of the following in terms of composition and texture: diorite, rhyolite, and basalt porphyry.
    Show Answer

    Diorite: Intrusive, intermediate, phaneritic
    Rhyolite: Extrusive, felsic, aphanitic
    Basalt: Extrusive, mafic, aphanitic

  • 5.1 - What is the geothermal gradient? Describe how the geothermal gradient compares with the melting temperatures of the mantle rock peridotite at various depths.
    Show Answer

    The geothermal gradient is the curve of temperature increase as depth increases. The melting point for peridotite is more than the gradient, so under normal conditions the mantle is solid.

  • 5.2 - Explain the process of decompression melting.
    Show Answer

    When the pressure on a rock is relieved, the atoms are not packed together tight enough to prevent melting and melting occurs.

  • 5.3 - What role do water and other volatiles play in the formation of magma?
    Show Answer

    The prescence of these volatiles will lower the melting point of a rock, creating magma more readily.

  • 5.4 - Name two plate tectonic settings in which you would expect magma to be generated.
    Show Answer

    Divergent boundaries and Convergent boundaries

  • 6.2 - Define Bowen's reaction series.
    Show Answer

    The sequence of silicate formation with the cooling of a silicate magma.

  • 6.3 - Describe the process of crystal settling and how it results in magmatic differentiation.
    Show Answer

    Crystal settling is when dense crystals sink to the bottom of a magma. This results in a magma with a different chemical composition as the silicate crystals now form an outer ring of rock.

  • 8.1 - What is meant by the term country rock?
    Show Answer

    It is the preexisting rock that intrusive magma forcefully displaces, or the host rock of a magma.

  • 8.2 - Describe dike and sill, using the appropriate terms from the following list: massive, discordant, tabular, and concordant.
    Show Answer

    Dike: Tabular, discordant
    Sill: Massive, concordant

  • 8.3 - Compare and contrast batholiths, stocks, and laccoliths in terms of size and shape.
    Show Answer

    Batholiths are the largest and irregular, stocks are like small batholiths. Laccoliths are smaller and are raised country rock from magma intrusion.

  • 8 - Describe two mechanisms by which mantle rock can melt without an increase in temperature. How do these magma-generating mechanisms relate to plate tectonics?
    Show Answer

    Adding volatiles (mostly water) - These lower the melting point of a rock. It occurs at convergent plate boundaries.
    Lowering pressure - The pressure compacting a rock is released, causing it to melt. It occurs along divergent plate boundaries and mantle plumes.

  • 11 - Each statement describes how an intrusive feature appears when exposed at Earth's surface due to erosion. Name the feature.

    3. A huge expanse of granitic rock forming a mountainous terrain tens of kilometers wide
    Show Answer

    a. Laccolith
    b. Dike
    c. Batholith
